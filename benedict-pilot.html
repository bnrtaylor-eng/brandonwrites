Regula Sancti Benedicti

A reader’s guide to the Rule of St. Benedict. The English text (Verheyen translation, public domain) appears on the left. Commentary notes on the right draw on Dom Paul Delatte’s Commentary on the Rule (1921, public domain) and the Saint Vincent Archabbey verse commentary (freely accessible online, drawing substantially on Terrence Kardong’s standard scholarly edition). Scripture references link to the Douay-Rheims version at BibleGateway.

Prologus
Prologue
Text (Verheyen, tr.)Commentary Notes

Listen, O my son, to the precepts of thy master, and incline the ear of thy heart, and cheerfully receive and faithfully execute the admonitions of thy loving Father, that by the toil of obedience thou mayest return to Him from whom by the sloth of disobedience thou hast gone away.

Prol. 1 — Listening

Delatte calls the opening address an intimate act of fatherhood, not legislation: Benedict “does not dream of imposing” but “appeals to your good will.” The imperative to listen is directed to the heart, not the ear. The Saint Vincent Archabbey commentary (drawing on Kardong) notes that Obsculta fuses hearing with “a suspenseful waiting, an intense psychological involvement” — a spiritual act. The first word of the Rule sets the tone for the whole document: receptivity precedes action.

  • Delatte, Commentary on the Rule (1921)
  • Saint Vincent Archabbey, Prologue Part 1

To thee, therefore, my speech is now directed, who, giving up thine own will, takest up the strong and most excellent arms of obedience, to do battle for Christ the Lord, the true King.

Prol. 2–3 — Obedience and Self-Will

Delatte identifies obedience as the central “weapon” of the spiritual life — active, martial, committed to Christ as King: “all intelligent beings are ranged in two camps, those who obey and those who obey not.” The SV Archabbey commentary (Kardong) distinguishes between departing (active disobedience) and drifting (passive neglect), calling the latter more dangerous because it is easily denied. Both commentators stress that renouncing self-will is not a single act but a daily renewal.

  • Delatte, Commentary on the Rule (1921)
  • Saint Vincent Archabbey, Prologue Part 1

In the first place, beg of Him by most earnest prayer, that He perfect whatever good thou dost begin, in order that He who hath been pleased to count us in the number of His children, need never be grieved at our evil deeds. For we ought at all times so to serve Him with the good things which He hath given us, that He may not, like an angry father, disinherit his children, nor, like a dread lord, enraged at our evil deeds, hand us over to everlasting punishment as most wicked servants, who would not follow Him to glory.

Prol. 4–5 — Prayer, Grace, and Filiation

Benedict’s insistence on prayer before every good work is, as Kardong notes, a preemption of Pelagianism: human effort does not precede grace but flows from it. Delatte reads the father/slave double image as invoking Roman paterfamilias law — disinheritance for sons, punishment for slaves — to underscore the stakes of unfaithfulness. The SV Archabbey commentary: “We do not act virtuously to prove ourselves to God.”

  • Delatte, Commentary on the Rule (1921)
  • Saint Vincent Archabbey, Prologue Part 1

Let us then rise at length, since the Scripture arouseth us, saying: “It is now the hour for us to rise from sleep” (Rom 13:11); and having opened our eyes to the deifying light, let us hear with awestruck ears what the divine voice, crying out daily, doth admonish us, saying: “Today, if you shall hear his voice, harden not your hearts” (Ps 94[95]:8). And what doth He say? “Come, children, hearken to Me; I will teach you the fear of the Lord” (Ps 33[34]:12). “Run whilst you have the light of life, lest the darkness of death overtake you” (Jn 12:35).

Prol. 8–18 — The Summons

The chain of scriptural quotations — Paul, Psalms, John, Isaiah — functions as a lectio divina in miniature. The SV Archabbey commentary reads “rise from sleep” as a daily conversion: “such is the command of the Master as we begin each day.” Kardong notes a possible allusion to the Transfiguration in the call to open our eyes to the light. Delatte emphasizes the tenderness of divine initiative: the Lord speaks before we call upon him (Is 58:9), reversing the ordinary sequence of petition and response.

  • Delatte, Commentary on the Rule (1921)
  • Saint Vincent Archabbey, Prologue Part 2

And the Lord seeking His workman in the multitude of the people saith: “Who is the man that desireth life?” (Ps 33[34]:13) God saith to thee: “If thou wilt have true and everlasting life, keep thy tongue from evil” (Ps 33[34]:14-15). And when you shall have done this, “My eyes shall be upon you”; and “before you shall call upon Me, I will say to you: Behold, here I am” (Is 58:9).

Prol. 14–18 — The Workman and the Answer

God seeks a workman “in the multitude of people” — the monastic vocation is implicit in the baptismal call common to all. The SV Archabbey commentary reads Benedict’s string of Psalm quotations as his own lectio: sharing the fruit of prayer with those who have heard the call. The answer to who wants life? is not an achievement but a posture: tongue, lips, peace.

  • Delatte, Commentary on the Rule (1921)
  • Saint Vincent Archabbey, Prologue Part 2

What, dearest brethren, can be sweeter to us than this voice of the Lord inviting us? See, in His loving kindness, the Lord showeth us the way of life.

Prol. 19–20 — Sweetness

The brief lyric digression — “What can be sweeter than this voice?” — marks a rhetorical shift from command to invitation. Delatte calls this the heart of the Prologue: the monastery is not primarily an institution of discipline but of love. Both commentators treat it as a hinge between the urgent summons of the preceding verses and the practical instruction that follows.

  • Delatte, Commentary on the Rule (1921)
  • Saint Vincent Archabbey, Prologue Part 2

If we desire to dwell in the tabernacle of His kingdom, we cannot reach it in any way, unless we run thither by good works. But let us ask the Lord with the Prophet, saying: “Lord, who shall dwell in Thy tabernacle, or who shall rest in Thy holy mountain?” (Ps 14[15]:1).

Prol. 21–25 — The Way and the Tabernacle

Benedict draws on Psalm 15 to frame the monastic life as an answer to a prior question: who shall dwell with God? The answer — the blameless, the just, the truth-speaker — is a portrait of character formed by grace, not earned by merit. Kardong notes that casting evil thoughts against Christ (echoing Ps 137:9) follows Cassian’s teaching on early resistance to temptation.

  • Delatte, Commentary on the Rule (1921)
  • Saint Vincent Archabbey, Prologue Part 2

After this question, brethren, let us listen to the Lord answering and showing us the way to this tabernacle, saying: “He that walketh without blemish and worketh justice; he that speaketh truth in his heart” (Ps 14[15]:2-3); who hath brought to naught the foul demon, and hath taken his evil thoughts whilst they were yet weak and dashed them against Christ; who fearing the Lord, glorify the Lord working in them, saying with the Prophet: “Not to us, O Lord, not to us, but to Thy name give the glory”; as the Apostle Paul said: “By the grace of God, I am what I am” (1 Cor 15:10); and “He that glorieth, let him glory in the Lord” (2 Cor 10:17).

Prol. 26–32 — Not to Us

The ethical summary of the long Psalm quotation: the good in us is not ours. Paul’s two lines — “By the grace of God, I am what I am” and “Let him who glories, glory in the Lord” — frame the Prologue’s entire ethical program. Delatte: “the holy action doth not perfect those that act, if they do not correspond with divine grace.” Both commentators read this as Benedict’s direct inheritance from Cassian and the desert tradition.

  • Delatte, Commentary on the Rule (1921)

Now, brethren, that we have asked the Lord who it is that shall dwell in His tabernacle, we have heard the conditions for dwelling there; and we shall fulfil the duties of that dweller, if we be humble, not in our own merits, but fearing God and keeping His commandments; knowing that the holy action doth not perfect those that act, if they do not correspond with divine grace.

Prol. 29–32 — Humility and Merit

Dwelling with God requires humility, not moral credit. The SV Archabbey commentary frames this as Benedict’s continuity with the entire patristic tradition: grace precedes, accompanies, and completes every act of virtue. Good works without inner transformation are insufficient.

  • Delatte, Commentary on the Rule (1921)
  • Saint Vincent Archabbey, Prologue Part 2

We are, therefore, about to found a school of the Lord’s service, in which we hope to introduce nothing harsh or burdensome. But if, for good reason, for the amendment of evil habits or the preservation of charity, there be some strictness of discipline, do not be at once dismayed and run away from the way of salvation. For as we advance in the religious life and faith, our hearts expand and we run the way of God’s commandments with unspeakable sweetness of love; so that never departing from His guidance, persevering in His doctrine in the monastery until death, we may by patience share in the sufferings of Christ, and deserve to be partakers of His kingdom.

Prol. 33–45 — Nothing Harsh; the School

The famous declaration — “we hope to introduce nothing harsh or burdensome” — is Benedict’s fundamental pastoral instinct. Delatte: the Rule moderates earlier, harsher monastic legislation out of care for the weak. The SV Archabbey commentary frames strictness as medicinal rather than punitive. The “narrow beginning” (Mt 7:14) gives way not to ease but to expansion of heart: difficulty is no longer experienced as burden when love grows. Kardong calls the closing promise of “unspeakable sweetness” the eschatological horizon of the whole Rule.

  • Delatte, Commentary on the Rule (1921)
  • Saint Vincent Archabbey, Prologue Part 2
